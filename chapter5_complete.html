Section 5.1
What is Big Data?
Big Data refers to extremely large and complex datasets that regular computer programs and databases cannot handle. It comes from three main sources: transactional data (e.g., online purchases), machine data (e.g., sensor readings), and social data (e.g., social media posts). To analyse and use Big Data effectively, special tools and techniques are required.

Small Data vs. Big Data

To understand Big Data, we must first understand Small Data. The contrast between them helps clarify what makes Big Data a unique challenge.

📊 Small Data

  • Easily comprehensible by people
  • Accessible, informative, and actionable
  • Ideal for individuals and small businesses
  • Can be managed with standard tools (Excel, RDBMS)
  • Used for everyday decision making
  • Example: A small store tracking daily sales to decide which products to restock

🌐 Big Data

  • Extremely large and complex datasets
  • Cannot be handled by traditional database systems
  • Requires special tools like Hadoop, Spark
  • Sources: transactional, machine, and social data
  • Enables innovation and better decision-making at scale
  • Example: Amazon & Netflix recommendations based on millions of user interactions

Sources of Big Data

🛒

Transactional Data

Data from business operations — online purchases, banking transactions, point-of-sale records, and customer orders. Generated continuously at enormous volume.

🤖

Machine Data

Data produced automatically by machines — sensor readings from IoT devices, server logs, industrial equipment metrics, GPS data, and medical devices.

📱

Social Data

Data from human interactions on digital platforms — social media posts, comments, likes, shares, images, videos, and online conversations.

📡

Streaming Data

Continuous real-time data flows from websites, apps, clickstreams, and live event feeds. Requires stream processing rather than batch processing.

📌 Scale of Big Data Today: According to latest estimates, approximately 328.77 million terabytes of data are created each day globally. Google alone handles over 40,000 search queries per second. Facebook processes over 100 billion messages per day. This scale is precisely why traditional tools fail and specialised Big Data technologies are needed.

Big Data is not uniform — it exists in three distinct forms based on structure, organisation, and how easily it can be processed. Understanding these types is essential for choosing the right tools and techniques for analysis.

Deep Dive — Each Data Type

1. Structured Data

Structured data is the most organised form — it fits neatly into rows and columns of a relational database. Every record follows the same format, making it easy to query using SQL. However, structured data represents only a small fraction (around 20%) of all data generated globally.

2. Semi-Structured Data

Semi-structured data has some organisational properties (like tags or markers) but does not conform fully to a relational model. JSON and XML are classic examples — they have a self-describing structure, but the fields and nesting can vary between records. Most modern web APIs exchange data in JSON format.

3. Unstructured Data

Unstructured data — the largest and fastest-growing category, making up approximately 80% of all data — has no predefined format. Text documents, images, audio recordings, and video files fall here. While difficult to process, unstructured data often holds the richest insights. Natural Language Processing (NLP) and Computer Vision are AI techniques specifically designed to extract value from this data.

Section 5.3
Advantages & Disadvantages of Big Data
Big Data is a key driver of modern innovation — it has changed how organisations analyse and use information. While it offers significant benefits, it also comes with challenges that affect its responsible adoption across industries.

Advantages of Big Data

🎯

Enhanced Decision Making

Big Data analytics empowers organisations to make data-driven decisions based on insights derived from large and diverse datasets — reducing guesswork and improving accuracy.

⚡

Improved Efficiency

By analysing vast data, businesses identify inefficiencies, streamline processes, and optimise resource allocation — leading to increased productivity and reduced waste.

👥

Better Customer Insights

Organisations gain deeper understanding of customer behaviour, preferences, and needs — enabling personalised marketing strategies and improved customer experiences.

🏆

Competitive Advantage

Leveraging Big Data analytics provides a competitive edge by enabling organisations to uncover market trends, identify opportunities, and stay ahead of competitors.

💡

Innovation & Growth

Big Data fosters innovation by facilitating new products, services, and business models based on insights from data analysis, driving business growth and expansion.

🏥

Healthcare Improvements

In healthcare, Big Data enables early disease detection, personalised treatments, drug discovery, and better management of patient records and outcomes.

Disadvantages of Big Data

⚠
Privacy & Security Concerns

The collection, storage, and analysis of large volumes of data raise significant privacy and security risks — including unauthorised access, data breaches, and misuse of personal information. Individuals often have little awareness of how their data is collected and used.

⚠
Data Quality Issues

Ensuring the accuracy, reliability, and completeness of Big Data is challenging. Data often comes from unstructured and heterogeneous sources, leading to potential errors, duplicates, and biases in analysis — producing misleading insights.

⚠
Technical Complexity

Implementing and managing Big Data infrastructure and analytics tools requires specialised skills and expertise. This creates significant technical challenges and resource constraints, especially for small and medium organisations.

⚠
Regulatory Compliance

Organisations must comply with data protection laws like GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation) and India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023. These require strict handling of personal data — compliance failures can result in heavy penalties.

⚠
Cost & Resource Intensiveness

The cost of acquiring, storing, processing, and analysing Big Data — along with hiring skilled data professionals — can be enormous. This is particularly challenging for smaller organisations with limited budgets and technical resources.

⚠️ Key Concern — Data Privacy: The DPDP Act 2023 (India) and GDPR (EU) both establish that individuals have rights over their personal data — including the right to know what is collected, the right to correct it, and the right to have it deleted. Any organisation collecting and processing Big Data must design its systems with these rights built in from the start, not added as an afterthought.
Section 5.4
Characteristics of Big Data — The 6Vs
The characteristics of Big Data refer to the defining attributes that distinguish large and complex datasets from traditional data. Originally described using the "3Vs" framework (Volume, Velocity, Variety), this has expanded to the "6Vs" framework to provide a more holistic view — adding Veracity, Value, and Variability.

🔺 The Original 3Vs

  • Volume — the sheer scale of data generated
  • Velocity — the speed of data creation and processing
  • Variety — the diversity of data formats and types
  • Introduced by Doug Laney (Gartner) in 2001
  • Still the foundation of any Big Data discussion

🔷 The Extended 6Vs

  • 3Vs + Veracity — data quality and trustworthiness
  • 3Vs + Value — the business insight extracted
  • 3Vs + Variability — inconsistency in data streams
  • Provides a complete framework for Big Data management
  • Helps organisations address all dimensions systematically

Detailed Explanation of All 6 Vs

📦

Volume

The enormous amount of data generated every day. As more people use online platforms, data volumes have grown exponentially. Typically, if data volume exceeds gigabytes — ranging to petabytes, terabytes, or exabytes — it falls into the realm of Big Data. According to latest estimates, 328.77 million terabytes are created globally each day.

⚡

Velocity

The speed at which data is generated, delivered, and analysed. In today's world, data is created and must be processed at tremendous speed. Google alone generates more than 40,000 search queries per second. Social media platforms receive millions of posts, likes, and interactions every minute, demanding real-time or near-real-time processing.

🎨

Variety

Big Data encompasses data in various formats — structured, unstructured, semi-structured, or highly complex. These range from simple numerical data to text, images, audio, and video. Storing and processing unstructured data through traditional RDBMS is challenging, yet this is where the richest insights often lie.

✅

Veracity

Veracity refers to the consistency, accuracy, quality, and trustworthiness of data. Not all data that undergoes processing holds value. It is essential to clean data effectively before storing or processing it — especially when dealing with massive volumes. Veracity addresses whether the data source is reliable and suitable for analytical models.

💎

Value

The goal of Big Data analysis is extracting business value. This is perhaps the most critical characteristic. Without obtaining valuable insights, the other characteristics hold little significance. Value refers to the tangible and intangible benefits that Big Data provides — improved decisions, new products, cost savings, and competitive advantage.

🔄

Variability

Variability refers to establishing that the contextualising structure of the data stream is regular and dependable, even under conditions of extreme unpredictability. It defines the need to derive meaningful data considering all possible circumstances — accounting for inconsistencies, changing meanings, and irregular patterns in data flows.

Section 5.5
Big Data Analytics
Big Data Analytics uses advanced analytic techniques against huge, diverse datasets — including structured, semi-structured, and unstructured data from different sources ranging from terabytes to zettabytes. It covers methodologies, tools, and practices for data collection, organisation, storage, and analysis to uncover patterns and address challenges.

Data Analytics vs. Big Data Analytics

📊 Data Analytics

  • Analyses datasets of any size (small to moderate)
  • Uses statistical analysis software
  • Data visualisation tools (Tableau, Power BI)
  • Relational Database Management Systems (RDBMS)
  • Suitable for structured, well-organised data
  • Example: Monthly sales analysis in Excel

🌐 Big Data Analytics

  • Advanced techniques for massive, diverse datasets
  • Distributed computing frameworks (Hadoop, Spark)
  • NoSQL databases (MongoDB, Cassandra)
  • Machine learning and AI integration
  • Handles all three data types simultaneously
  • Example: Netflix's recommendation algorithm

Types of Big Data Analytics

📋

Descriptive Analytics

"What happened?" — Summarises historical data to understand past events. Uses data aggregation and mining. Example: Monthly sales reports, website traffic summaries.

🔍

Diagnostic Analytics

"Why did it happen?" — Examines data to understand the cause of outcomes. Uses drill-down, correlations, and data discovery. Example: Why did sales drop in Q3?

📈

Predictive Analytics

"What will happen?" — Uses statistical models and machine learning to forecast future outcomes. Example: Predicting customer churn, weather forecasting, fraud detection.

🎯

Prescriptive Analytics

"What should we do?" — Recommends actions based on predictive insights. Uses simulation and optimisation. Example: Supply chain optimisation, personalised treatment plans.

4 Global Trends Driving Big Data Analytics

1
Moore's Law — Exponential Computing Power

The exponential growth of computing power, as per Moore's Law, has enabled the handling and analysis of massive datasets. Processing power that once required supercomputers is now available on consumer hardware, driving the evolution of Big Data Analytics.

2
Mobile Computing — Data at Your Fingertips

With the widespread adoption of smartphones and mobile devices, access to vast amounts of data is now available everywhere. Mobile usage enables real-time connectivity and continuous data collection from users across the globe — 24/7.

3
Social Networking — User-Generated Data at Scale

Platforms such as Facebook, Instagram, YouTube, and Pinterest facilitate extensive networks of user-generated content, interactions, and data sharing — leading to the generation of massive datasets perfect for analysis and insight extraction.

4
Cloud Computing — On-Demand Infrastructure

This paradigm shift in technology infrastructure allows organisations to access hardware and software resources remotely via the Internet on a pay-as-you-go basis — eliminating the need for extensive on-premises investments and enabling instant scale-up for Big Data workloads.

💡 Key Tools for Big Data Analytics: Apache Hadoop (distributed file system and processing), Apache Spark (in-memory fast processing), Cassandra and MongoDB (NoSQL databases for unstructured data), Tableau (data visualisation), and SAS (statistical analysis). Each tool is optimised for a specific aspect of the Big Data pipeline.

Working Process of Big Data Analytics
Big Data analytics involves a systematic, multi-step process: collecting, processing, cleaning, and analysing enormous datasets to improve organisational operations. Each step is critical — errors at any stage propagate forward and compromise the final insights.

ML models · Clustering · Classification · Visualisation

Step 1 — Gather Data

Each company has a unique approach to data collection. Organisations can now collect structured and unstructured data from various sources, including cloud storage, mobile applications, IoT sensors, social media platforms, and transactional systems. The challenge at this step is identifying which data sources are relevant to the business question being investigated.

Step 2 — Process Data

Once data is collected and stored, it must be processed properly to get accurate results on analytical queries, especially when it is large and unstructured. Two main processing approaches exist:

Batch Processing

  • Processes large blocks of data over a scheduled time period
  • Suitable for non-time-sensitive analysis
  • Used for end-of-day reports, monthly trend analysis
  • Tools: Apache Hadoop MapReduce, Hive
  • Lower cost but higher latency

Stream Processing

  • Processes small batches of data in near-real time
  • Shortens delay between data collection and analysis
  • Used for fraud detection, live dashboards, IoT monitoring
  • Tools: Apache Spark Streaming, Apache Kafka
  • Higher cost but lower latency — faster decision-making

Normalisation

  • Scales numerical values to a specific range (e.g., 0–1 or –1–1)
  • Makes values comparable across different scales
  • Improves the performance of machine learning algorithms
  • Essential before applying models like KNN, SVM, Neural Networks
  • Applied in Orange using the Preprocess widget

Step 3 — Clean Data

Scrubbing all data, regardless of size, improves quality and yields better results. Correct formatting and elimination of duplicate or irrelevant data are essential. Erroneous and missing data can lead to inaccurate insights — the famous "garbage in, garbage out" principle. Data cleaning methods include:

📊

Average (Mean) Imputation

Replace missing numerical values with the mean of the available values in that column. Simple and effective for data with a normal distribution.

📈

Mode Imputation

Replace missing values with the most frequently occurring value (mode). Best suited for categorical variables where mean is not applicable.

🔢

Fixed Value

Replace missing values with a specific constant (e.g., 0, "Unknown"). Appropriate when domain knowledge justifies using a particular default value.

🎲

Random Value

Replace missing values with a random value drawn from the column's distribution. Preserves statistical properties while filling gaps.

Step 4 — Analyse Data

Once data is in a clean, usable state, advanced analytics processes can turn Big Data into actionable insights. Common analytical techniques include:

Big Data Analytics with Orange Data Mining — Practical Workflow

Orange Data Mining is a visual, widget-based tool that makes Big Data analytics accessible without heavy coding. The following workflow demonstrates all four steps using the Heart Disease dataset.

Step 1 — Gather Data in Orange

Use the File widget to load data into Orange. Load the built-in Heart Disease dataset. Study the features carefully:

Features (Input Variables)

  • age — patient's age in years
  • gender — male or female
  • chest pain type — 4 categories
  • rest_sbp — resting systolic blood pressure
  • cholesterol — serum cholesterol (mg/dl)
  • rest_ecg — resting ECG results
  • max_hr — maximum heart rate achieved

Target Variable

  • diameter_narrowing — the outcome to predict
  • Value 1 = significant narrowing of arteries (risk factor for heart disease)
  • Value 0 = healthier arteries with minimal or no narrowing
  • This is a binary classification problem

Step 2 — Process Data (Normalisation)

Connect the Preprocess widget to the File widget. Select "Normalise Features" and choose an interval of 0–1. Connect a Data Table widget to verify — all numerical values should now be scaled between 0 and 1. This ensures no single feature dominates due to its scale (e.g., age vs. cholesterol).

Step 3 — Clean Data (Handle Missing Values)

Connect the Impute widget to handle missing values. Select an imputation strategy (e.g., Average/mean). Connect a Data Table widget to confirm — all missing values should now be filled. In our dataset, some values are missing and must be imputed before any model can be applied.

Step 4 — Analyse Data (Build & Evaluate a Model)

4a
Build a Logistic Regression Model

Drag and drop the Logistic Regression widget. Connect it to the cleaned and normalised data output. This creates a model that will learn from the patient features to predict diameter narrowing.

4b
Test the Model — Test & Score Widget

Add the Test and Score widget. Connect it to both the Logistic Regression widget (learner) and the processed data. Select Cross-Validation as the validation method for robust performance estimation.

4c
Generate Predictions — Predict Widget

Connect the Predict widget to the Test and Score widget to view the actual predictions generated by the Logistic Regression model — comparing predicted vs. actual outcomes for each patient record.

🔗 Complete Orange Workflow: File → Preprocess (Normalise) → Impute (Handle Missing) → Logistic Regression + Test & Score → Predict. This pipeline represents the complete 4-step Big Data Analytics process — from raw data to validated model predictions — all within Orange's visual interface.
Section 5.7
Mining Data Streams
A data stream is a continuous, real-time flow of data generated by various sources — sensors, satellite images, Internet and web traffic, financial markets, and more. Unlike traditional stored datasets, data streams are potentially infinite and must be processed as they arrive.

What is Data Stream Mining?

Mining data streams refers to the process of extracting meaningful patterns, trends, and knowledge from a continuous flow of real-time data. Unlike traditional data mining which analyses stored, static datasets, stream mining processes data as it arrives — without storing the entire dataset. This has several important implications:

Key Characteristics

  • Data arrives continuously and potentially without end
  • Cannot store the entire stream — must process on the fly
  • Each data item is processed once (or a limited number of times)
  • Decisions must be made quickly with incomplete information
  • Patterns may change over time (concept drift)

Sources of Data Streams

  • IoT sensors — temperature, pressure, motion
  • Satellite imagery — weather, vegetation, disaster monitoring
  • Internet traffic — clickstreams, web access logs
  • Financial markets — stock prices, currency exchange rates
  • Social media — Twitter trending topics, live event reactions
  • Network monitoring — server logs, intrusion detection

Traditional vs. Stream Mining

  • Traditional: Entire dataset available upfront
  • Stream: Data arrives one item or batch at a time
  • Traditional: Multiple passes through data allowed
  • Stream: One pass only — limited memory
  • Traditional: Batch algorithms (Hadoop)
  • Stream: Incremental algorithms (Spark Streaming)

Real-World Example — Website Data Streams

Websites typically receive continuous streams of data daily. For instance, a sudden spike in searches for "election results" on a search engine on a particular day might indicate that elections were recently held in a region — or highlight the level of public interest. This kind of real-time pattern detection, impossible with traditional batch analytics, is the power of data stream mining.

⚠️ Challenge — Concept Drift: One of the most significant challenges in data stream mining is concept drift — the statistical properties of the data change over time. For example, a fraud detection model trained on last year's transaction patterns may fail on this year's data as fraudsters adapt their methods. Stream mining algorithms must detect and adapt to these changes automatically.

Applications of Data Stream Mining

💳

Fraud Detection

Banks analyse each credit card transaction in real time as it occurs — flagging suspicious patterns instantly, before fraudulent transactions are completed.

📡

Network Monitoring

IT systems monitor network traffic streams to detect unusual patterns that could indicate a cyberattack, denial-of-service attack, or security breach in real time.

🌡️

IoT Monitoring

Industrial equipment sensors stream data continuously. Anomaly detection on these streams enables predictive maintenance — fixing equipment before it fails.

📊

Stock Market Analysis

Financial algorithms process millions of trades per second to detect arbitrage opportunities, monitor volatility, and execute algorithmic trades in microseconds.

Section 5.8
Future of Big Data Analytics
The future of Big Data Analytics is highly influenced by several key technological advancements that will shape how data is processed and analysed. These trends are not distant possibilities — many are already emerging and will become mainstream in the coming decade.
⚡

Real-Time Analytics

Real-time analytics will allow businesses to process data instantaneously, providing immediate insights for decision-making. This enables actions based on live data — monitoring customer behaviour, tracking supply chain activities, and responding to events as they unfold.

🧠

Advanced Predictive Models

Predictive analytics will evolve with the integration of more sophisticated machine learning and AI algorithms, enabling organisations to forecast trends and behaviours with far greater precision and accuracy than current models allow.

⚛️

Quantum Computing

Quantum computing promises to revolutionise Big Data analytics by offering unprecedented processing power. Quantum computers will solve complex optimisation and simulation problems much faster than classical computers — enabling analyses currently considered computationally impossible.

🌐

Edge Computing & IoT

Rather than sending all data to the cloud, edge computing processes data at the source — near sensors and devices. This reduces latency and bandwidth costs, making real-time IoT analytics feasible at massive scale.

🤖

Automated ML (AutoML)

AutoML tools will automate the selection, tuning, and deployment of machine learning models — democratising Big Data analytics and making it accessible to organisations without large teams of data scientists.

🔐

Privacy-Preserving Analytics

Techniques like federated learning and differential privacy will enable organisations to extract insights from sensitive data without ever accessing the raw data — resolving the tension between analytics and privacy.

Section B — True or False
TF 1. Big Data refers to datasets that are too large to be processed by traditional database systems. (True / False)
True. Big Data is specifically defined by the fact that it cannot be effectively managed, processed, or analysed by traditional database tools like standard relational databases (RDBMS). This is exactly what makes it "Big" — necessitating special frameworks like Hadoop, Spark, and distributed computing architectures.
TF 2. Structured data is the primary type of data in Big Data analytics, making up the majority of datasets. (True / False)
False. Unstructured data makes up approximately 80% of all data generated globally. Structured data, while the easiest to process, represents only a small fraction of total data. The majority of Big Data consists of unstructured data — images, videos, audio files, social media posts, emails — which requires special tools like NLP and Computer Vision to process.
TF 3. Veracity in Big Data refers to the trustworthiness and reliability of data. (True / False)
True. Veracity is the V of the 6Vs framework that addresses the quality, accuracy, consistency, and trustworthiness of data. Not all data collected is reliable — data may be incomplete, inconsistent, or inaccurate. Veracity emphasises the importance of data cleaning and validation before analytics to ensure insights are based on trustworthy information.
TF 4. Real-time analytics involves processing and analysing data as it is generated, without any delay. (True / False)
True. Real-time analytics (also called stream processing) processes data immediately as it is generated — with near-zero latency. This is in contrast to batch processing, which accumulates data over a period and analyses it at a scheduled time. Real-time analytics is used for fraud detection, live monitoring, and instant recommendations.
TF 5. A CSV file is an example of structured data. (True / False)
True. A CSV (Comma-Separated Values) file is an example of structured data — it organises data in rows and columns with a consistent format. Each row represents one record and each column represents one field. CSV files can be directly imported into spreadsheets and relational databases for easy querying and analysis.
Section C — Short Answer Questions
SQ 1. Define Big Data. What makes it different from traditional data?
Definition: Big Data refers to extremely large and complex datasets that cannot be effectively managed, processed, or analysed by traditional database systems and software tools.

What makes it different from traditional data:

1. Scale: Big Data operates at scales of petabytes, terabytes, and exabytes — far beyond what traditional databases can handle. An estimated 328.77 million terabytes are created globally every day.

2. Variety: Big Data includes structured, semi-structured, and unstructured formats — not just neatly organised rows and columns. Traditional databases primarily handle structured data.

3. Velocity: Big Data often arrives in real-time or near-real-time streams (e.g., 40,000+ Google searches per second), whereas traditional data is typically collected in batches.

4. Tools required: Traditional tools like Excel or standard RDBMS cannot handle Big Data. Special distributed computing frameworks like Apache Hadoop and Spark, along with NoSQL databases, are needed.

In summary, Big Data is not just "a lot of data" — it is data characterised by the 6Vs (Volume, Velocity, Variety, Veracity, Value, Variability) that fundamentally changes how we must approach storage, processing, and analysis.
SQ 2. Explain the 6Vs of Big Data with examples for each V.
1. Volume — The massive amount of data generated. Example: ~328.77 million terabytes created globally each day. Facebook stores over 100 petabytes of user data.

2. Velocity — The speed of data generation and the need for rapid processing. Example: Google processes 40,000+ search queries per second; Twitter generates 6,000+ tweets per second. Financial markets require microsecond-level transaction processing.

3. Variety — The diversity of data formats: structured (spreadsheets, transaction records), semi-structured (JSON, XML files), and unstructured (images, videos, emails, social media posts). A single hospital generates all three types simultaneously.

4. Veracity — The trustworthiness and quality of data. Example: Social media data contains fake accounts, bots, and spam that must be filtered before analysis. In healthcare, incorrect patient data could lead to dangerous medical decisions.

5. Value — The business benefit extracted from analysis. Example: Netflix's recommendation engine (built on Big Data analytics) is estimated to save $1 billion per year by reducing subscriber churn. Without extracting this value, the other Vs are meaningless.

6. Variability — Inconsistencies and changing patterns in data streams. Example: The meaning of the word "python" in social media posts varies — it could refer to the programming language, the snake, or the comedy group Monty Python. Context changes over time and must be accounted for.
SQ 3. Explain the four-step working process of Big Data Analytics.
Big Data Analytics follows a four-step systematic process:

Step 1 — Gather Data: Collect structured and unstructured data from various sources including cloud storage, mobile apps, IoT sensors, social media platforms, and transactional systems. Each organisation develops a unique data collection strategy based on its business requirements. The challenge here is identifying which data sources are relevant and ensuring the data collected is comprehensive enough to support meaningful analysis.

Step 2 — Process Data: Raw collected data must be prepared for analysis through either Batch Processing (large data blocks processed over time — suitable for non-time-sensitive analysis) or Stream Processing (small batches in near-real time — for faster decision-making). Normalisation is also applied at this stage to scale numerical values to a standard range (e.g., 0–1), ensuring no feature dominates the model due to scale differences.

Step 3 — Clean Data: Data quality issues must be addressed before analysis. This involves: correcting formatting errors, eliminating duplicate records, and handling missing values using methods such as mean imputation (replace missing with average), mode imputation (replace with most frequent value), or fixed value replacement. Without cleaning, erroneous data propagates into analysis and produces misleading insights.

Step 4 — Analyse Data: Apply advanced analytics techniques to the prepared data — including K-Means clustering for segmentation, Logistic Regression or Decision Trees for prediction, and visualisation tools (Scatter Plots, Heat Maps, Box Plots) for pattern discovery. Tools like Tableau, Apache Hadoop, Cassandra, MongoDB, and Orange Data Mining are commonly used at this stage to extract actionable insights.
SQ 4. Differentiate between Structured, Semi-Structured, and Unstructured data with two examples of each.
Structured Data:
Data that is highly organised into a predefined format — stored in rows and columns in a relational database. It has a fixed schema that all records conform to, making it easily searchable using SQL.
Examples: (1) A customer database with fields: CustomerID, Name, Email, Phone, City — every record follows the same structure. (2) A bank's transaction records with fields: Date, AccountNumber, Amount, TransactionType — uniform and queryable.

Semi-Structured Data:
Data that has some organisational properties (like tags or markers) but does not conform fully to a relational model. It has a self-describing structure that may vary between records, making it flexible but harder to query than structured data.
Examples: (1) A JSON file containing product information where different products may have different attribute fields. (2) An XML configuration file where elements follow a hierarchical but variable structure.

Unstructured Data:
Data that has no predefined format, schema, or organisation. It cannot be directly stored in traditional relational databases and requires special tools (NLP for text, Computer Vision for images) to extract meaning.
Examples: (1) Audio recordings of customer service phone calls — no schema, raw audio waveform. (2) Social media posts including text, images, hashtags, and mentions — highly variable, contextual, and human-generated.
Section D — Long Answer Questions
LQ 1. A healthcare company is using Big Data analytics to manage patient records, predict disease outbreaks, and personalise treatments. However, the company is facing challenges regarding data privacy. What are the potential risks to patient privacy, and how can they be mitigated?
Context: Healthcare Big Data — including electronic health records, genomic data, medical imaging, and wearable device data — is among the most sensitive personal information that exists. Its misuse can cause irreversible harm to individuals.

Potential Privacy Risks:

1. Unauthorised Access & Data Breaches: Large centralised healthcare databases are prime targets for cyberattacks. A breach can expose millions of patient records — including diagnoses, medications, and personal details — to criminal misuse, discrimination, or public exposure.

2. Re-identification from Anonymised Data: Even when patient data is "anonymised" (names removed), combining it with other available data (location, age, rare conditions) can re-identify individuals. Studies have shown that 87% of Americans can be uniquely identified using just zip code, birth date, and gender.

3. Secondary Use Without Consent: Patient data collected for one purpose (treatment) may be shared with or sold to pharmaceutical companies, insurance companies, or researchers without patients' knowledge or consent — violating the principle of informed consent.

4. Discriminatory Use by Insurers & Employers: If health data reaches insurers or employers, individuals with pre-existing conditions could face higher premiums, denial of coverage, or employment discrimination.

5. Regulatory Non-Compliance: Failing to comply with India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act (DPDP) 2023 or GDPR (for international data) can result in heavy financial penalties and reputational damage.

Mitigation Strategies:

1. Data Encryption: Encrypt all patient data both at rest and in transit using strong encryption standards. This ensures data is unreadable even if accessed without authorisation.

2. Role-Based Access Control (RBAC): Ensure only authorised personnel can access specific data — a radiologist needs imaging data but not billing records. Apply the principle of least privilege.

3. Differential Privacy: Add mathematical noise to datasets before analysis — enabling patterns to be discovered without revealing any individual's exact data.

4. Federated Learning: Train AI models on patient data at each hospital locally — never moving raw data to a central server. Only model updates (gradients) are shared, not the underlying data.

5. Explicit Informed Consent: Obtain clear, specific consent from patients before using their data for any purpose beyond direct care — with the right to withdraw consent at any time.

6. Regular Audits: Conduct regular security audits, penetration testing, and data governance reviews to proactively identify and address vulnerabilities.

Conclusion: The benefits of Big Data in healthcare — earlier disease detection, personalised treatments, better resource allocation — are enormous. However, these must be balanced with robust privacy protection. Privacy should be designed into the system from the start (Privacy by Design), not bolted on as an afterthought.
