Computer Vision — The Big Picture
Computer Vision (CV) is a field of Artificial Intelligence that enables machines to interpret and understand information from the visual world — images, videos, and live feeds — just as humans use their eyes and brain. The machine captures visual data, processes it, and makes decisions or extracts useful information from it.

Section 3.1
What is Computer Vision?
Computer Vision is a branch of AI that trains computers to interpret and understand visual information from the world — photographs, videos, real-time camera feeds. It mimics the functionality of the human visual system, allowing machines to identify objects, faces, text, scenes, and activities, and act on what they "see."

Human Vision vs. Machine Vision

Understanding the difference between how humans and machines process visual information is fundamental to appreciating both the power and the limitations of Computer Vision.

AspectHuman VisionMachine Vision (CV)
InputLight hits the retina via the eye's lensDigital image / video frame (pixels)
Processing unitVisual cortex in the brainCPU / GPU running algorithms or neural networks
Recognition speed~150 ms for familiar objectsCan be <10 ms with optimized hardware
Context understandingIntuitive — uses memory, emotion, experienceStatistical — pattern matching from training data
Performance in darknessVery poorUses infrared / thermal — unaffected by light
ConsistencyVaries with tiredness, attention, emotionConsistent across millions of images
Adversarial robustnessVery hard to fool a humanSmall pixel changes can fool a CV model
Learning mechanismBiological experience over yearsTraining on labelled datasets
💡 Key Insight: Machines do NOT "see" the way humans do. A machine receives an array of numbers (pixel values) and learns statistical patterns. It does not understand meaning the way we do — this is why CV systems can fail on images that look completely normal to a human.

How an Image is Represented — Pixels

Every digital image is made up of pixels (picture elements). Each pixel stores colour information as a number. Understanding this is the foundation of all Computer Vision.

⬛

Grayscale Image

Each pixel is a single value from 0 (black) to 255 (white). A 100×100 grayscale image is a matrix of 10,000 numbers.

🎨

Colour (RGB) Image

Each pixel has 3 values — Red, Green, Blue — each from 0–255. A 100×100 colour image = 30,000 numbers.

📐

Resolution

Width × Height in pixels. A Full HD image is 1920×1080 = ~2 million pixels. Higher resolution = more data to process.

🔢

Channels

Grayscale has 1 channel, RGB has 3 channels. Some medical or satellite images have 4+ channels (e.g. infrared).

🔬 A 5×5 Grayscale Image — Each cell shows pixel intensity (0=black, 255=white)

↑ Each cell = 1 pixel. The number is its intensity. A full image is millions of such numbers arranged in a grid.

🎨 RGB Colour Image = 3 Stacked Grids (Channels)

A colour image is three separate grids stacked together — one each for Red, Green, and Blue. Each pixel = (R, G, B) values.

Step 1 — Image Pre-processing

Raw images are rarely ready for direct analysis. Pre-processing cleans and standardises the data before the model sees it. This step critically affects model accuracy.

📏

Resizing

Neural networks require a fixed input size (e.g. 224×224). Images are resized to this dimension before feeding into the model.

📊

Normalisation

Pixel values (0–255) are scaled to a range like 0–1 or –1 to 1. This speeds up training and improves stability.

🌫️

Noise Reduction

Filters like Gaussian blur smooth out unwanted random variation (noise) that could mislead the model.

⬛

Grayscale Conversion

Some tasks don't need colour. Converting RGB → Grayscale reduces data from 3 channels to 1, cutting computation by 3×.

🔄

Data Augmentation

Training images are flipped, rotated, cropped, and colour-shifted to artificially expand the dataset and improve generalisation.

✂️

Cropping / Padding

Region of interest (ROI) cropping focuses on the relevant area. Padding adds border pixels to maintain size after processing.

Step 2 — Feature Extraction

Features are the meaningful patterns in an image — edges, corners, textures, blobs — that allow a system to distinguish one object from another. Feature extraction transforms raw pixel data into a compact, meaningful representation.

Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)

The most powerful CV systems today are built on Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). Unlike traditional feature engineering where humans define what to look for, CNNs learn features automatically from the training data.

🧠 Simplified CNN Architecture

CNN layers learn increasingly complex features: edges → shapes → object parts → full objects → classification

🔑 How CNN learning works: In the early layers, the network learns to detect simple patterns like horizontal/vertical edges. Deeper layers combine these into shapes, then object parts, and finally recognisable objects. This hierarchy of features is automatically learned from thousands of labelled training images — no human hand-codes the features.
Section 3.3
Applications of Computer Vision
Computer Vision is one of the most commercially deployed AI technologies today. It is found in healthcare, agriculture, retail, transport, manufacturing, security, and entertainment — fundamentally transforming how industries operate and deliver value.

Healthcare & Medical Imaging

🩺

Disease Detection

CV models analyse X-rays, MRI scans, and CT images to detect cancer, diabetic retinopathy, pneumonia, and other conditions — often matching or exceeding radiologist accuracy.

🔬

Pathology Analysis

Microscope slide images are analysed to detect abnormal cells in tissue samples. CV automates what was previously manual, time-consuming work.

💊

Surgery Assistance

Robotic surgery systems use CV to guide surgical instruments with sub-millimetre precision during minimally invasive procedures.

👁️

Eye Disease Screening

Google's DeepMind trained a system on retinal scans to detect over 50 eye conditions from a single fundus photograph.

Autonomous Vehicles & Transport

1
Perception Layer

Cameras, LiDAR, and radar capture the environment. CV processes camera feeds to detect pedestrians, vehicles, cyclists, road markings, traffic signs, and obstacles in real time.

2
Lane & Road Detection

CV identifies lane boundaries, road edges, and driving surfaces even in challenging conditions like rain, fog, or night driving.

3
Traffic Sign Recognition

Classification models recognise speed limit signs, stop signs, traffic lights, and other regulatory signage in real time and feed this to the vehicle's decision system.

4
Parking & Manoeuvring

Around-view cameras with CV enable automated parking, obstacle avoidance during low-speed manoeuvring, and parking space detection.

Agriculture — Precision Farming

Crop Monitoring

Yield Estimation

Section 3.4
Challenges in Computer Vision
Despite remarkable progress, Computer Vision systems still face fundamental challenges that prevent them from reliably matching human visual intelligence in all scenarios. Understanding these challenges is essential for responsible deployment.

Key Challenge Categories

🗺️ Computer Vision Challenges Overview

Detailed Challenge Analysis

Domain ShiftModel trained on one dataset performs poorly on data from a different contextMedical model trained on USA data fails in IndiaTransfer learning, domain adaptation
3D from 2DCameras capture 2D; real world is 3D — depth is lostMisinterpret distances, sizes, 3D relationshipsStereo cameras, LiDAR fusion, depth sensors
⚠️ The "Clever Hans" Problem in CV: ML models can learn spurious correlations in training data. A famous CV experiment found a model "detecting" wolves — but it was actually detecting snow in the background, as all wolf training images had snowy backgrounds. This is a critical limitation: models learn shortcuts, not true understanding.

The Data Challenge

CV models are data-hungry. A typical image classifier needs millions of labelled images. Labelling is expensive, time-consuming, and introduces human error. This creates a significant barrier:

Problem: Data Scarcity

  • Rare diseases → very few medical images
  • Edge cases (accidents, unusual weather) are underrepresented
  • Labelling by experts is expensive
  • Privacy rules restrict data sharing (medical, personal)

Solutions Being Used

  • Transfer Learning — reuse models trained on ImageNet
  • Data Augmentation — artificially expand training data
  • Synthetic Data — generate realistic images using GANs
  • Self-supervised Learning — learn from unlabelled images
  • Active Learning — smart selection of which images to label

Ethical Considerations in Computer Vision
Computer Vision technologies — especially facial recognition and surveillance — raise profound ethical, legal, and social questions. As these systems become more pervasive, understanding their potential for harm is as important as understanding their technical capabilities.

The Big Five Ethical Issues

1 — Privacy & Surveillance

CV-powered cameras can identify, track, and build profiles of individuals without their knowledge or consent. Facial recognition in public spaces enables mass surveillance — the ability to track everyone's movements at all times. This fundamentally alters the nature of public space.

Example: China's Social Credit System uses thousands of CV-enabled cameras to track citizen behaviour in public. Critics argue this creates a surveillance state that chills free speech, assembly, and dissent.

2 — Bias & Algorithmic Discrimination

CV models trained on non-representative data can produce systematically biased outcomes. The consequences are not merely technical — they affect real people's lives.

Study / CaseFindingConsequence
MIT Media Lab "Gender Shades" (2018)Commercial facial analysis tools had error rates of 0.8% for light-skinned men vs. 34.7% for dark-skinned womenDiscriminatory outcomes in deployment
Amazon Rekognition (2018)Misidentified 28 US Congress members as criminals; disproportionately misidentified people of colourACLU raised serious misuse concerns
UK Police Facial Recognition (2019)Independent review found 81% of matches were false positivesMultiple innocent people wrongly stopped

3 — Consent & Autonomy

When facial images are scraped from social media or captured in public without consent, individuals lose control over their own biometric data. Unlike passwords, you cannot change your face. This makes biometric data uniquely sensitive and its misuse irreversible.

🔑 GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation) in the European Union classifies biometric data as "special category" data requiring explicit, informed consent before collection and processing. Many other regions are developing similar protections.

4 — Misuse & Weaponisation

CV technologies can be misused by authoritarian governments to suppress political opposition, by stalkers to track victims, by criminals to commit identity fraud, or in autonomous weapons systems. The same technology that helps a doctor diagnose cancer can help a regime identify protesters.

🚨

Deepfakes

CV-based generative models can superimpose faces onto videos convincingly. Used for disinformation, non-consensual intimate imagery, and political manipulation.

🎯

Autonomous Weapons

Lethal autonomous weapon systems (LAWS) using CV to identify and engage targets without human authorisation raise profound legal and moral questions.

🏃

Gait Recognition

Beyond faces, CV can identify people by their walking pattern from a distance — making physical disguise ineffective against surveillance.

💼

Workplace Monitoring

CV-based employee monitoring tracks keystrokes, eye movements, facial expressions, and time away from desk — raising serious workplace dignity concerns.

5 — Accountability & Explainability

When a CV system makes a consequential decision — refusing a visa, flagging someone for police investigation, denying insurance — who is accountable if it is wrong? Deep learning models are often "black boxes" — even their creators cannot fully explain why a specific decision was made.

Explainable AI (XAI) is an active research area aiming to make AI decisions interpretable. Techniques like Grad-CAM can produce visual "heatmaps" showing which regions of an image most influenced a classification decision, helping identify if the model is using the right features — or wrong ones.

The Debate — Should Facial Recognition be Banned?

🟢 Arguments FOR regulation/use

  • Can find missing children and trafficking victims
  • Secure, convenient identity verification
  • Critical for public safety in terrorism prevention
  • Already regulated — similar to fingerprints or DNA
  • Technology will improve and bias will reduce over time

🔴 Arguments FOR a ban

  • Proven racial bias causes discriminatory harm now
  • Chilling effect on legitimate free expression and protest
  • Consent is impossible in public deployment
  • Scope creep — systems built for safety used for control
  • Mistakes in high-stakes contexts (criminal justice) are unacceptable
📌 Global Response: San Francisco, Boston, and several other US cities have banned government use of facial recognition. The EU AI Act (2024) classifies real-time remote biometric identification in public as "high risk" with severe restrictions. India is developing a National Facial Recognition System covering 1.4 billion people — with ongoing debate about safeguards.

Principles for Responsible CV

1
Necessity & Proportionality

Only deploy CV when there is a clear, specific need. The capability to collect data does not create the right to do so. The benefit must outweigh the privacy cost.

2
Transparency

Individuals must be informed when CV systems are in use. Organisations must be clear about what data is collected, how it is processed, and who can access it.

3
Fairness & Representation

CV systems must be tested across all demographic groups before deployment. Systems with proven disparate impact must not be deployed until bias is addressed.

4
Human Oversight

In consequential decisions (criminal justice, employment, medical), CV should assist humans — never replace human judgement. Meaningful appeal mechanisms must exist.

5
Data Minimisation & Security

Collect only what is needed, retain for the shortest necessary time, and protect biometric data with the highest security standards.

Section B — Short Answer Questions
SQ 1. Explain the difference between Object Detection and Image Segmentation with examples.
Object Detection locates and identifies objects within an image using bounding boxes — rectangular outlines drawn around each detected object, along with a label and confidence score. It answers "What is in the image and where is it approximately?"

Example: A self-driving car's camera detects 3 pedestrians and 2 vehicles — each surrounded by a labelled box.

Image Segmentation goes a step further by labelling every single pixel in the image with a class. This creates a precise pixel-level mask of each object's exact boundary.

Example: In medical imaging, a tumour is precisely outlined at the pixel level so a surgeon knows its exact size, shape, and boundary with surrounding tissue.

Key difference: Detection → approximate location (box); Segmentation → exact boundary (pixel mask). Segmentation is more computationally expensive but provides finer-grained information.
SQ 2. What is the significance of pre-processing in the CV pipeline? Explain any three pre-processing steps with their purpose.
Significance: Pre-processing standardises raw visual data — cleaning, resizing, and transforming it into a consistent format that the model can efficiently learn from. Poor pre-processing directly causes poor model performance even if the architecture is excellent. It is the "garbage in, garbage out" stage of the pipeline.

Three Key Pre-processing Steps:

1. Resizing: Neural networks require a fixed input size (e.g., 224×224 pixels for many ImageNet models). All images — regardless of their original resolution — must be resized to this exact dimension. Without this, the model cannot process them.

2. Normalisation: Pixel values (0–255) are scaled to a smaller range like 0–1 or –1 to 1. This prevents large numerical values from dominating gradients during training, speeds up convergence, and improves model stability.

3. Data Augmentation: Training images are artificially modified — flipped horizontally, rotated, cropped, brightness-adjusted — to create new training examples. This expands the dataset and forces the model to learn features that are robust to these real-world variations, reducing overfitting.
SQ 3. Discuss two major ethical concerns in the deployment of facial recognition technology, with real-world examples.
1. Algorithmic Bias and Discrimination:
CV facial recognition systems have been shown to perform significantly worse on individuals with darker skin tones, particularly dark-skinned women. The MIT Media Lab's 2018 "Gender Shades" study found error rates as high as 34.7% for dark-skinned women compared to 0.8% for light-skinned men in leading commercial products.

Real example: Amazon's Rekognition tool misidentified 28 US members of Congress as criminals — with the misidentifications disproportionately affecting people of colour. When such systems are used in law enforcement, these errors can result in innocent people being wrongfully stopped, questioned, or even arrested.

2. Privacy and Surveillance:
Facial recognition deployed in public spaces allows individuals to be tracked and identified without their knowledge or consent. Unlike opting out of social media, you cannot avoid your own face in public. This enables authoritarian regimes to monitor political opponents, suppress protest, and build profiles on ordinary citizens.

Real example: China's Social Credit System uses an extensive network of CV-enabled cameras to monitor citizen behaviour in public spaces and influence social and economic opportunities based on behaviour scores. Critics argue this represents unprecedented state surveillance incompatible with fundamental freedoms. Several democratic cities — including San Francisco and Boston — have preemptively banned government use of facial recognition in response to these concerns.
