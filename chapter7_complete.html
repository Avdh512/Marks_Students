Section 7.1
Introduction to Generative AI
Generative AI, a facet of artificial intelligence, creates diverse content like audio, text, images, and more, aiming to generate new data resembling its training samples. It utilises machine learning algorithms to learn from existing datasets. Examples include ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, and DALL-E.
📝

Creates coherent, contextually relevant text based on prompts. Powers chatbots, content creation, story writing, and code generation tools like ChatGPT and Gemini.

🎨

🎵

Section 7.2
Working of Generative AI
Generative AI learns patterns from data and autonomously generates similar samples. It operates within deep learning, employing neural networks to understand intricate patterns. Models such as GANs and VAEs facilitate tasks like image and text generation.

7.2.1 Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)

A GAN consists of two competing networks — a Generator that creates fake data samples and a Discriminator that evaluates whether samples are real or fake. Through adversarial training, both networks improve until the generator produces highly realistic outputs.

GAN Architecture — Generator vs. Discriminator Competition

GAN Applications: Image generation, style transfer, data augmentation, face synthesis, and video creation. GANs excel at producing visually realistic outputs and are the technology behind most deepfakes.

7.2.2 Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)

VAEs consist of an Encoder that compresses data into a latent space, and a Decoder that reconstructs new data from the latent space. Unlike GANs, VAEs focus on capturing underlying data patterns for structured generation.

VAE Architecture — Encoder → Latent Space → Decoder

GANs — Best For

  • Creating visually realistic images and videos
  • Style transfer and face synthesis
  • Deepfake and data augmentation tasks
  • High-quality creative output generation

VAEs — Best For

  • Structured data generation tasks
  • Anomaly detection in datasets
  • Filling in missing information
  • More stable, interpretable latent spaces
Section 7.3
Generative & Discriminative Models
Machine learning algorithms can be broadly categorised into two types: Discriminative models define class boundaries and excel at classification tasks. Generative models comprehend the underlying data distribution and can generate entirely new samples.

Models Uses competition (GANs) or pattern-based guessing (VAEs) to create new things Learns rules to separate categories and recognise patterns — e.g., dog vs. cat classification
Training Focus Understands what makes data unique and creates new data that is similar but different Focuses on learning decision boundaries to separate different categories of data

Algorithm Examples GAN, VAEs, LLMs, DBMs, Autoregressive models, Gaussian discriminant analysis, Naïve Bayes Logistic Regression, Decision Trees, SVM, Random Forest
📘 Simple Analogy: Think of discriminative models as a sorter — they decide if an email is spam or not. Generative models are like an artist — they can create brand new emails or images from scratch by understanding patterns in existing data.

Section 7.5
LLM — Large Language Models
A Large Language Model (LLM) is a deep learning algorithm that performs a variety of Natural Language Processing (NLP) tasks — generating and classifying text, answering questions conversationally, and translating between languages. LLMs are "large" because they are trained on massive datasets containing trillions of words.

7.5.1 Transformers in LLMs

Transformers are a revolutionary neural network architecture that enables efficient learning of complex language patterns. The attention mechanism in transformers allows LLMs to understand relationships between words across very long text sequences.

7.5.4 Limitations & Risks of LLMs

Limitations

  • High computational resource requirements — high response time and costs
  • Prioritise natural language fluency over accuracy — may generate factually incorrect information with high confidence (hallucinations)
  • May memorise specific details rather than generalise, leading to poor adaptability on new tasks

Risks

  • Trained on internet text — may exhibit societal biases and raise data privacy concerns
  • Using sensitive data in training can inadvertently reveal confidential information
  • Vulnerability to prompt injection attacks — crafted inputs can lead to harmful or illogical outputs

7.5.5 Case Study: LLaMA — A Publicly Trained Powerhouse

Demystifying LLaMA — Meta AI's Open Approach to LLMs

Background: Traditionally, LLMs are trained on massive proprietary datasets, limiting transparency and accessibility. LLaMA, developed by Meta AI, stands out for its approach to training data and architecture design.

Disruptive Training Approach: LLaMA leverages publicly available text and code from the internet. This focus on open data fosters transparency within the research community and allows for wider accessibility. LLaMA's developers also implemented efficient training techniques requiring less computational power than comparable LLMs.

Flexible Multi-Model Design: LLaMA is released in various sizes — from 7 billion to 65 billion parameters — allowing users to choose the model that best suits their computational resources and task requirements. Smaller models work on lower-power devices; larger models handle complex tasks.

Impressive Results: Despite efficient training with publicly available data, LLaMA benchmarks competitively against some larger LLMs — particularly in text summarisation and question answering. Applications span personalised education, content creation, and research assistance.

Section 7.7
Ethical & Social Implications of Generative AI
Generative AI, with its ability to create realistic content such as images, videos, and text, brings a multitude of ethical and social considerations. Its potential for misuse raises significant concerns about trust, fairness, intellectual property, and transparency in digital society.
🎭

Deepfake Technology

Tools like DeepFaceLab and FaceSwap raise concerns about digital content authenticity. Deepfake algorithms generate compelling fake images, audio, and videos, jeopardising media trust and spreading misinformation. They have been used to superimpose individuals' faces onto content without consent, causing reputational damage.

⚖️

Bias & Discrimination

Generative AI models have demonstrated biases that disproportionately affect certain demographic groups. AI-powered hiring platforms (like HireVue) may inadvertently discriminate against underrepresented groups by relying on historically biased data, perpetuating social inequalities.

📋

Plagiarism

Presenting AI-generated content as one's own raises ethical questions about intellectual property rights and academic integrity. If AI output significantly resembles copyrighted material, it could infringe copyright laws and lead to legal consequences for the user.

🔍

Transparency

Disclosing the use of AI-generated content is essential to uphold ethical standards. Failure to disclose AI use can erode trust and undermine the credibility of research, journalism, and scholarly work. Always be transparent about AI assistance.

Points to Remember

✅ Ethical Guidelines for Using Generative AI:
• Be cautious and transparent when using Generative AI — always disclose AI involvement in your work.
• Respect copyright — avoid presenting AI output as entirely your own original creation.
• Verify accuracy — AI-generated information may be incorrect or biased; always cross-check with reliable sources.
• Consult your teacher or institution for specific guidelines on acceptable AI use.

Citing Sources with Generative AI

1
Treat AI as Author

Cite the tool name (e.g., Gemini, ChatGPT) and label it "Generative AI tool" in the author position of your citation.

2
Date it Correctly

Use the date you received the AI-generated content, not the tool's release date or training cutoff date.

3
Show Your Prompt (Optional)

Briefly mention the prompt you gave the AI to provide context for readers about what was requested and generated.

Activity 6
Creating a Customised Chatbot with Gemini API

Large e-commerce companies like Amazon deploy AI-powered chatbots to handle customer queries — answering questions about order status, return policies, and product information. In education, healthcare, and research, customised chatbots enhance productivity. Let's build one using Python and the Gemini API!

  1. Obtain API Key: Visit Google AI Studio (aistudio.google.com), click "Get API key," then "Create API key." Copy and save it securely.
  2. Set Up Python Environment: Install the Gemini SDK: pip install -q -U google-generativeai
  3. Import Libraries: Import the google.generativeai library to interact with the Gemini API.
  4. Initialise with API Key: Configure the library by passing your API key as the value of GOOGLE_API_KEY.
  5. Choose Model: Select the Gemini model — gemini-pro for text-only, gemini-pro-vision for text and images combined.
  6. Create Chat Session: Start a chat session and send messages to receive AI-generated conversational responses.
chatbot_gemini.py
# Step 2: Install the Gemini Python SDK
# pip install -q -U google-generativeai

# Step 3: Import the generative AI library
import google.generativeai as genai

# Step 4: Initialise with your API key
genai.configure(api_key="YOUR_GOOGLE_API_KEY")

# Step 5: Choose the model
# gemini-pro: optimized for text-only prompts
# gemini-pro-vision: for text-and-images prompts
model = genai.GenerativeModel('gemini-pro')

# Step 6: Create a chat session
chat = model.start_chat(history=[])

# Function to send messages and get responses
def chat_with_gemini(user_message):
    response = chat.send_message(user_message)
    return response.text

# Run an interactive chatbot loop
print("🤖 Gemini Chatbot is ready! Type 'quit' to exit.")
while True:
    user_input = input("\nYou: ")
    if user_input.lower() == 'quit':
        break
    response = chat_with_gemini(user_input)
    print(f"\n🤖 Gemini: {response}")

B. True / False
1. Generative AI can only generate text and images, not audio or video.
🔴 False — Generative AI can generate text, images, audio, AND video. Each modality has dedicated tools: Meta AI's Voicebox for audio, Google's Lumiere for video, etc.
2. GANs involve competition between a Generator and Discriminator network.
✅ True — GANs consist of two competing networks: the Generator creates fake samples, the Discriminator evaluates real vs. fake. This adversarial training drives both networks to improve.
3. Discriminative models generate new data samples similar to the training data.
🔴 False — Discriminative models focus on identifying class boundaries and classifying data. Generative models are responsible for creating new data samples.
4. VAEs are generative models that can be used for tasks such as image generation.
✅ True — Variational Autoencoders are generative models used for image generation, anomaly detection, and filling in missing information through their encoder-latent space-decoder architecture.
5. Deepfake technology is an application of Generative AI that can create realistic fake videos.
✅ True — Deepfake technology uses Generative AI (specifically GANs) to create highly realistic fake images, audio, and videos, often superimposing one person's likeness onto another's without consent.
6. Large Language Models like GPT cannot generate human-like text.
🔴 False — LLMs like GPT are specifically designed to generate human-like text. They are trained on massive text datasets to understand and produce coherent, contextually relevant language.

D. Short Answer Questions
1. What is Generative AI?
Generative AI is a facet of artificial intelligence that creates diverse new content — text, images, audio, and video — that resembles its training samples. It uses machine learning algorithms to learn from existing datasets and generate new data autonomously. Examples include ChatGPT (text), DALL-E (images), MusicLM (audio), and Lumiere (video).
2. How does Generative AI work?
Generative AI learns patterns from data using deep learning and neural networks. GANs use a generator-discriminator competition: the generator creates fake samples from random noise, while the discriminator evaluates real vs. fake. Through adversarial training, both improve until the generator produces realistic outputs. VAEs use an encoder to compress data into a latent space, then a decoder to reconstruct new, similar data.
3. What distinguishes generative models from discriminative models?
Discriminative models focus on defining class boundaries, making them suitable for classification tasks like spam detection or image recognition. Generative models seek to understand the underlying data distribution and create new samples. Simple analogy: discriminative models are sorters, generative models are creators. Examples — discriminative: SVM, Decision Trees; generative: GANs, VAEs, LLMs.
4. What are the limitations and risks of Large Language Models?
Limitations: High computational resource requirements; tendency to generate factually incorrect information with high confidence (hallucinations); may memorise specific details rather than generalise. Risks: Training on internet text leads to biases; data privacy concerns when processing personal information; vulnerability to prompt injection attacks that generate harmful outputs; potential for spreading misinformation at scale.
5. What ethical considerations surround Generative AI?
Key ethical concerns: (1) Deepfakes — AI-generated fake media that spreads misinformation and violates privacy; (2) Bias & Discrimination — AI models that amplify societal biases in hiring, facial recognition, etc.; (3) Plagiarism — presenting AI-generated content as original work; (4) Transparency — failing to disclose AI use erodes trust in academic and professional contexts. Always be transparent, verify outputs, and respect intellectual property.
