Section 8.1
Introduction to Storytelling
"Once upon a time, there lived a King…" "On a dark night, when it was raining…" "Long long ago…" — these familiar sentences spark interest, enthusiasm, and curiosity in all of us. Stories have been a part of our lives since ancient times — from cave paintings to digital media. They are a way to share our imaginations, experiences, and thoughts with others.

What Are Stories?

Stories are a valuable form of human expression. They connect us closely with one another and transport us to different places and times. They can be folk tales, fairy tales, fables, and real-life stories. Each type creates a sense of connection — folk tales, in particular, strengthen our sense of belonging and help establish our identity.

According to the dictionary, a 'story' is a 'factual or fictional narrative' — meaning it tells about an event that can be true or made up, in a way that the listener experiences or learns something. Stories can be used to share information, experiences, or viewpoints.

Why is Storytelling a Powerful Tool?

✨

Captivates Audiences

Storytelling generates interest and draws people to the narrative — keeping them engaged and focused throughout. Attention is the scarcest resource in the information age.

💡

Communicates Meaning

Stories make complex ideas more accessible and understandable — packaging abstract concepts in a concrete, relatable form that the human brain naturally processes and remembers.

❤️

Evokes Emotion

Stories inspire us, trigger emotions, and motivate us to take action. Emotional connection is what makes the difference between information that is heard and information that is acted upon.

🌍

Builds Connection

Stories connect us closely with one another across time, culture, and geography — creating shared understanding and community between storyteller and audience.

The 5 Elements of a Story

Every well-crafted story contains five core elements that work together to create a complete, engaging narrative:

1
Characters

The people, animals, or objects featured in the story. They perform the actions and drive the story forward. Without compelling characters, there is no story — characters give the audience someone to connect with and root for.

2
Plot / Setting

Setting refers to the time or location in which the story takes place. Plot refers to the sequence of events of the story — the chain of cause and effect that carries the narrative from beginning to end.

3
Conflict

The problem or situation the characters are dealing with. Conflict drives the story forward — without conflict, there is no tension, no stakes, and no reason for the audience to remain engaged. It is the engine of every story.

4
Resolution

The end of the story where the characters arrive at a situation that resolves the conflict. It is the stage after the climax — the peak or height of any story — where loose ends are tied and the audience gains closure.

5
Insights

The ability to have a clear, deep, and sometimes sudden understanding of a complicated problem or situation. Insights are the takeaway — what the audience learns, understands, or feels differently about after experiencing the story.

🔑 Storytelling Forms: Storytelling can take many forms — oral (spoken word, podcasts), digital (video, social media, interactive web), and written (books, reports, articles). Each form uses the same five elements but adapts them to the medium and audience. In data storytelling, all three forms are often combined.

Section 8.3
Essential Elements of Data Storytelling
The three key elements of Data Storytelling are Data, Narrative, and Visuals. Each element is powerful on its own — but the real impact comes from skillfully blending all three together. When you combine the right visuals and narrative with the right data, you create a data story that can influence and drive change.
🔢

Data

Basic facts or raw facts about any entity. Data is the primary building block of every data story. It serves as the foundation for the narrative and visual elements. Without accurate, reliable data, the entire story collapses — no matter how compelling the visuals or narrative are.

📝

Narrative

The structure or storyline crafted to present insights from data in a clear, engaging, and informative manner. It involves identifying and organising key information in a linear and coherent fashion — ensuring the storyline is crisp and contextual. A well-defined narrative allows the audience to understand the significance of insights.

📊

Visuals

Pictorial representations of data using various graphs, charts, and diagrams. Visuals help convey complex information more clearly and effectively — enabling the audience to see trends, patterns, and anomalies that would be invisible in raw numbers, facilitating better understanding and interpretation.

How the Three Elements Interlink

🔗 The Three Elements — Interaction & Overlap

The overlapping centre — where all three elements meet — is where a true Data Story is created.

Data + Narrative

  • Explaining data observations helps the audience understand how the data behaves in different situations
  • Narrative gives context to why certain insights are generated
  • Without narrative, data is just numbers — hard to interpret or act on

Data + Visuals

  • Visualising data through charts and graphs allows the audience to see the data from a different perspective
  • Helps stakeholders analyse and make the right decisions faster
  • Patterns and trends invisible in tables become obvious in a chart

Narrative + Visuals

  • Combining narrative and visuals can engage or even entertain an audience
  • Visuals without narrative lack direction; narrative without visuals can be abstract
  • Together, they make data memorable and persuasive
💡 Key Insight: Each element — data, narrative, or visuals — can be powerful on its own. You can achieve some success with a compelling story, an interesting statistic, or impressive data visualisation. However, the real impact comes from skillfully blending all three in a data story. When you combine the right visuals and narrative with the right data, you create a data story that can influence and drive change.
Section 8.4
Narrative Structure — Freytag's Pyramid
Most stories follow a common arc — a protagonist who faces a complication goes on a journey of resolving a difficulty before returning to normal life. Building on Aristotle's simple model, Gustav Freytag developed a more robust narrative framework — a pyramid-based dramatic structure with five key stages that captures the arc and progression of any story.

🏛️ Freytag's Pyramid — The 5-Stage Dramatic Arc

The 5 Stages Explained

1
Introduction (Exposition)

The beginning of the story when the setting is established and main characters are introduced. It provides the audience with ample background information to understand what is going to happen. In data storytelling, this is where you introduce the context — the business problem, the dataset, and why this story matters.

2
Rising Action

The series of events that build up to the climax of the story — increasing tension and complication. In data storytelling, this is where you present the data analysis — revealing trends, patterns, and anomalies that build toward the central insight. Each chart or finding adds to the momentum.

3
Climax — The Turning Point

The most intense or important point within the story — an event in which the fortune of the protagonist turns for better or worse. In data storytelling, this is the most critical insight — the "aha moment" where the data reveals something surprising, significant, or actionable that reframes the audience's understanding.

4
Falling Action

The rest of the events that unravel after the main conflict has occurred, but before the final outcome is decided. In data storytelling, this is where you interpret the implications of the key insight — exploring what the data means, what questions it raises, and what alternatives exist.

5
Conclusion (Resolution)

The conclusion of the story where all conflicts are resolved and outstanding details are explained. In data storytelling, this is your call to action — the recommendation, the decision, or the change you are proposing based on the data insights uncovered throughout the story.

Freytag's Pyramid in Data Storytelling

In the context of data storytelling, Freytag's Pyramid can be used as a framework to structure the presentation of data and insights in a way that captivates the audience's attention and guides them through the narrative journey. The "Data Storytelling Arc" applies these same five stages to real-world data presentations:

Pyramid StageData Storytelling EquivalentExample
IntroductionSet the scene — business context, dataset overview, the question being asked"Our school dropout rates have been changing over the last decade. Here is the dataset we analysed."
Rising ActionBuild with data — show trends, comparisons, supporting evidence"Dropout rates declined sharply between 1995 and 2005. Here are three charts showing the pattern."
ClimaxThe key insight — the unexpected finding or critical takeaway"The sharpest decline occurred precisely after the Mid-Day Meal Scheme launched in 1995."
Falling ActionInterpret — explore causes, implications, alternative explanations"This correlation holds across states with higher scheme implementation rates."
ConclusionCall to action — recommendation or decision based on insights"Expanding the MDMS to more schools could further reduce dropout rates by an estimated 12%."
Section 8.5
Types of Data & Matching Visualisations
Data visualisation is a powerful way to show context. Data charts can reveal crucial deviations or affinities in the data that lead to insights. The key is choosing the right chart for the right data type — using the wrong visualisation can obscure insights rather than reveal them.
Data TypeVisualisationDescription & When to Use
Text Data 📝 Word Cloud Visual representation of word data where word size indicates frequency and importance. Use when you want to show which words, themes, or topics appear most often in a body of text — e.g., customer feedback analysis.
Mixed Data 🗃️ FacetGrid A multi-axes grid with subplots to visualise distribution and relationships between variables. Use when you need to compare how a relationship changes across categories — e.g., sales patterns across different regions.
Numeric Data 📈 Line Graph Illustrates data changing over time, useful for trends. Best when showing continuous change — e.g., daily temperature readings, monthly revenue over years.
📊 Bar Chart Compares data between categories using vertical or horizontal bars. Use when comparing discrete groups — e.g., sales by product category, exam scores by subject.
🥧 Pie Chart Circular chart illustrating numerical proportions as slices of a whole. Use only when comparing parts to a whole — e.g., market share breakdown, budget allocation.
⚫ Scatter Plot Visualises relationships and trends between two variables. Use when exploring correlation — e.g., age vs. cholesterol level, advertising spend vs. revenue.
📉 Histogram Represents distribution of continuous data through bars of varying heights. Use when showing how data is distributed — e.g., frequency of exam score ranges.
Categorical/Heat 🌡️ Heat Map Compares data across categories using colour intensity to identify strong and weak values. Use for dense multi-variable comparisons — e.g., correlation matrices, website click patterns.
Stock / Financial 🕯️ Candlestick Chart A visual aid for decision-making in stock, forex, commodity, and option trading. Each "candle" shows opening, closing, high, and low prices for a time period.
Geographic Data 🗺️ Map Chart Utilises geographical maps to display data points or statistical information associated with specific locations. Use for regional comparisons — e.g., population density, election results by state.

Choosing the Right Visualisation

📅

Time-Based Data

Use Line Graphs for continuous change over time. They clearly show trends, seasonal patterns, and the rate of change — ideal for revenue, temperature, or user growth over months and years.

🆚

Comparing Categories

Use Bar Charts for comparing discrete groups. They are intuitive and easy to read — great for comparing sales by region, scores by subject, or performance across different products.

🔗

Relationships Between Variables

Use Scatter Plots to reveal correlations. If you want to know whether two variables are related — e.g., does more study time lead to better grades? — scatter plots show this immediately.

🧩

Parts of a Whole

Use Pie Charts only when the proportional relationship to a whole matters. But use sparingly — human eyes struggle to accurately compare slice sizes. Bar charts often communicate the same information more clearly.

⚠️ Common Visualisation Mistakes: Using 3D charts (distorts proportion), starting bar chart axes at non-zero values (exaggerates differences), using too many colours (creates confusion), choosing pie charts with many categories (impossible to read), and using line charts for non-continuous categorical data. Always ask: "Does this visual make the insight clearer or more confusing?"
Section 8.6
Steps to Create a Story Through Data
If data collected is represented in just a series of graphs and charts, it will not serve the purpose of any organisation. It should be communicated with proper narrative, context, meaning, relevance, and clarity. The narrative should take the focus of the audience to the correct spot and not miss out on important facts. To find compelling stories in datasets, four steps must be followed.
1
Collect the Data and Organise It

Gather relevant, reliable data from appropriate sources. Organise it systematically — clean missing values, remove duplicates, and ensure the data is accurate and complete. The quality of your story depends entirely on the quality of your data. Define the question your story will answer before collecting data.

2
Use Proper Visualisation Tools to Visualise the Data

Select the right chart or graph type for your data (refer to Section 8.5). Use tools like MS Excel, Tableau, Power BI, or Python (matplotlib/seaborn) to create clear, clean visualisations. Ensure your visualisation is accurate, properly labelled, and does not mislead.

3
Observe the Relationships Between the Data

Analyse the visualisations — look for trends, patterns, correlations, outliers, and anomalies. Ask: What is changing over time? Are two variables correlated? Where is the surprise in the data? This analytical step is where the key insight — the heart of your story — is discovered.

4
Create a Simple Narrative Hidden in the Data

Craft a clear, concise narrative that communicates the insight to your audience. Structure it using Freytag's Pyramid — introduce context, build with supporting data, reveal the key insight, interpret its implications, and end with a call to action. The narrative should be crisp, contextual, and tailored to your audience's level of data literacy.

Case Study — Mid-Day Meal Scheme & Dropout Rates

Example: Using available data on student enrolment, attendance, and dropout rates, create a compelling data story that explores the impact of the Mid-Day Meal Scheme (MDMS) since its launch in 1995.

Step 1 — Collect & Organise

  • Gather state-wise dropout rate data from 1985–2020
  • Collect MDMS implementation dates and coverage data per state
  • Include enrolment and attendance figures as supporting data
  • Clean data — handle missing years, standardise formats

Step 2 — Visualise

  • Line graph: Dropout rate over time (1985–2020) — shows trend clearly
  • Bar chart: Compare dropout rates before vs. after MDMS by state
  • Scatter plot: MDMS coverage % vs. dropout rate reduction
  • Map chart: Geographic visualisation of MDMS impact across states

Steps 3 & 4 — Observe & Narrate

  • Key insight: Dropout rates declined most sharply post-1995 in states with high MDMS adoption
  • Correlation between MDMS coverage and attendance improvement
  • Narrative: "The Mid-Day Meal Scheme didn't just feed students — it kept them in school"
  • Call to action: Expand scheme coverage to increase enrolment further

Case Study — Covid Vaccine Data Story

A second classic example: using vaccination rate data and COVID-19 case numbers over time, create a data story showing the impact of the vaccine rollout.

Step 1 — Prepare Data in MS Excel

  • Date column, daily new cases column, cumulative vaccination % column
  • Organise chronologically from vaccine launch date
  • Include multiple waves of the pandemic for full context

Step 2 — Visualise with Line Chart

  • Dual-axis line chart: Cases on left axis, vaccination % on right axis
  • Time on x-axis spanning pre-vaccine to post-vaccine period
  • Visual clearly shows the inverse relationship as vaccination rises

Step 3 — Narrative

  • "Covid Vaccine — Gives a Ray of Hope"
  • Introduction: pandemic context and challenge
  • Climax: the turning point when cases begin declining as vaccination rates cross key thresholds
  • Conclusion: clear evidence that vaccination drives case reduction
Section 8.7
Ethics in Data Storytelling
Data storytelling is a powerful tool — and with great power comes great responsibility. Each of the three key elements of data storytelling (Data, Narrative, and Visuals) presents unique ethical challenges that must be addressed to ensure responsible and trustworthy storytelling. A misleading data story can cause real harm — to individuals, organisations, and society.
✅

Accuracy

Ensure that the data is accurate, reliable, and truthful. Avoid manipulating data to support a predetermined narrative. Present all relevant data — not just the subset that supports your conclusion. Cherry-picking data points is a form of deception.

🔍

Transparency

Clearly cite the sources of the data, methods used for analysis, and any limitations or biases in the dataset. Be transparent about the story's purpose and potential conflicts of interest. The audience deserves to know how the data was collected and interpreted.

🔐

Respect for Privacy

Protect the privacy of individuals and groups represented in the data. Avoid sharing personal or sensitive information without consent. Anonymise individual data before including it in a public-facing data story. Follow data protection regulations (GDPR, DPDP Act 2023).

Ethical Pitfalls to Avoid

Ethical IssueWhat It Looks LikeHow to Avoid It
Misleading VisualisationsStarting a bar chart y-axis at a non-zero value to exaggerate differences; using a 3D pie chart that distorts proportionsAlways start axes at zero; use 2D charts; include proper scale labels
Cherry-Picking DataOnly showing the time period or subset of data that supports your conclusion, ignoring contradicting evidencePresent the full dataset; acknowledge anomalies; show all relevant trends
Correlation ≠ CausationClaiming that two correlated variables have a direct causal relationship without evidence (e.g., "ice cream sales cause drowning")Use careful language — "associated with" rather than "causes"; acknowledge confounding variables
Privacy ViolationDisplaying individual-level data that identifies specific people without consent; using personal health or financial data publiclyAggregate data; anonymise individuals; obtain informed consent; follow data protection law
Biased NarrativeFraming the narrative to lead the audience to a predetermined conclusion that serves the storyteller's interests rather than the truthPresent multiple perspectives; acknowledge uncertainty; let the data guide the narrative, not vice versa
Missing ContextPresenting a data point without the context needed to interpret it correctly — e.g., "crime increased by 100%" (from 1 case to 2)Always provide baseline numbers, sample sizes, and relevant context for all statistics
⚠️ Remember: A data story does not just happen on its own — it must be curated and prepared by someone for the benefit of other people. The person crafting the story holds significant power over how the audience perceives reality. With that power comes the ethical obligation to represent data honestly, completely, and in a way that respects both the data subjects and the audience.

Conclusion — The Power of Data Stories

When we effectively combine the right insights with the right narratives and visuals, we can communicate data in a manner that can inspire change. Data stories help other people understand a problem, risk, or opportunity in a meaningful way that compels them to act on it.

📌 Data Storytelling Defined: Data storytelling is a persuasive, structured approach for communicating insights using narrative elements and explanatory visuals to inform decisions and drive changes. It is not just a technical skill — it is a communication skill, a design skill, and above all, an ethical responsibility.

Section B — True or False
TF 1. The main purpose of data visualisation is to hide the data from the audience. (True / False)
False. The main purpose of data visualisation is the opposite — to reveal and communicate data clearly to the audience. Visualisation transforms complex numerical data into intuitive visual forms (charts, graphs, maps) that make trends, patterns, and relationships visible and understandable. Hiding data would be an ethical violation, not the purpose of the tool.
TF 2. Data storytelling involves presenting insights and findings in a compelling narrative format. (True / False)
True. This is the core definition of data storytelling. It is the practice of translating complex data and analytics into a compelling narrative that is easily understandable and relatable to various audiences — combining data, visuals, and narrative to communicate insights effectively.
TF 3. Data storytelling involves presenting raw data without any analysis or interpretation. (True / False)
False. Data storytelling is fundamentally about interpretation, not just presentation. Presenting raw data without analysis would simply be data reporting. Data storytelling requires analysing the data, identifying insights, crafting a narrative, and selecting appropriate visualisations — the entire process of turning numbers into meaning.
TF 4. In Freytag's Pyramid, the Climax comes before the Rising Action. (True / False)
False. In Freytag's Pyramid, the correct order is: Introduction → Rising Action → Climax → Falling Action → Conclusion. The Climax is the peak of the pyramid and comes after Rising Action has built up the tension and stakes. The Rising Action is the series of events that leads to the climax — not the other way around.
TF 5. Combining data, narrative, and visuals creates a more impactful data story than using just one element alone. (True / False)
True. Each element — data, narrative, or visuals — can be powerful on its own. However, the real impact comes from skillfully blending all three. When you combine the right visuals and narrative with the right data, you create a data story that can influence and drive change in a way that no single element can achieve alone.
Section C — Short Answer Questions
SQ 1. Define Data Storytelling. How does it differ from regular data visualisation?
Data Storytelling is the art and practice of translating complex data and analytics into a compelling narrative that is easily understandable and relatable to various audiences. It is a persuasive, structured approach for communicating insights using narrative elements and explanatory visuals to inform decisions and drive changes.

Difference from Data Visualisation:

Data Visualisation is the pictorial representation of data using charts, graphs, and diagrams. It is only ONE of the three elements of data storytelling — the visual component.

Data Storytelling goes further by combining three elements together: (1) Data — the factual foundation, (2) Visuals — the graphical representation, and (3) Narrative — the structured storyline that provides context, meaning, and a call to action. A chart alone shows "what" — a data story explains "what, why, and what to do about it." Visualisation is a tool; data storytelling is a complete communication strategy.
SQ 2. Name and explain the three essential elements of Data Storytelling.
The three essential elements of Data Storytelling are:

1. Data: Basic facts or raw facts about any entity. Data is the primary building block of every data story — it serves as the foundation for the narrative and visual elements. Without accurate, reliable data, the story has no credibility. Data can be qualitative or quantitative, from any number of sources.

2. Narrative: The structure or storyline crafted to present insights derived from data in a clear, engaging, and informative manner. It involves identifying and organising key information in a linear and coherent fashion — ensuring the storyline is crisp and contextual. A well-defined narrative allows the audience to understand the significance of insights and how they relate to the broader context of the analysis.

3. Visuals: Pictorial representations of data using various graphs, charts, and diagrams. They help convey complex information more clearly and effectively — enabling the audience to see trends, patterns, and anomalies in the data. Visuals serve as a scene of the data presented in graphical format, facilitating better understanding and interpretation of the insights being communicated.
SQ 3. Describe Freytag's Pyramid and its application in Data Storytelling.
Freytag's Pyramid is a narrative framework developed by Gustav Freytag, building on Aristotle's simple model. It describes the arc or progression of a story through five key stages using a pyramid shape: Introduction → Rising Action → Climax → Falling Action → Conclusion.

The Five Stages:
1. Introduction: Setting and characters established; background provided
2. Rising Action: Series of events building tension toward the climax
3. Climax: The most intense point; fortune of the protagonist turns
4. Falling Action: Events unravel after the conflict; heading toward resolution
5. Conclusion: All conflicts resolved; outstanding details explained

Application in Data Storytelling:
Freytag's Pyramid is used to structure data presentations — Introduction sets the business context and question; Rising Action presents the data analysis and evidence; Climax reveals the key insight or "aha moment" in the data; Falling Action interprets the implications; and Conclusion delivers the recommendation or call to action. This structure captivates audience attention and guides them through the narrative journey from problem to insight to action.
SQ 4. Name the correct graph type for each data situation: (a) word frequency in customer reviews, (b) stock price movements, (c) sales comparison across 5 regions, (d) relationship between study hours and exam scores.
(a) Word frequency in customer reviews → Word Cloud
A Word Cloud visually represents text data where word size indicates frequency and importance. It immediately shows which words appear most often — making it perfect for analysing customer feedback, social media posts, or any text corpus.

(b) Stock price movements → Candlestick Chart
A Candlestick Chart is specifically designed for financial data — showing opening, closing, high, and low prices for each time period. It is the standard visualisation for stock, forex, commodity, and option trading analysis.

(c) Sales comparison across 5 regions → Bar Chart
A Bar Chart compares data between discrete categories using bars. It is the ideal choice for comparing values across a fixed number of groups — making the relative size of each region's sales immediately apparent.

(d) Relationship between study hours and exam scores → Scatter Plot
A Scatter Plot visualises the relationship and trends between two variables. Each point represents one student (their study hours and their score) — the pattern of points reveals whether there is a positive correlation between the two variables.
Section D — Long Answer Questions
LQ 1. Why has Data Storytelling become very powerful nowadays? Discuss with reference to the changing data landscape and business needs.
Data storytelling has become extremely powerful today for several interconnected reasons rooted in both technological change and evolving business needs:

1. Explosion of Data: We now generate approximately 328.77 million terabytes of data every day. Organisations are drowning in data but starving for insights. Data storytelling is the bridge that translates overwhelming quantities of data into meaningful, actionable insights that decision-makers can actually use.

2. Non-Technical Decision-Makers: Most business decisions are made by leaders who are not data scientists. Data storytelling packages complex analytical findings in accessible narrative and visual forms that non-technical stakeholders can understand, trust, and act upon — without needing to understand the underlying methodology.

3. Information Overload: In today's information-saturated environment, attention is scarce. A well-crafted data story cuts through the noise by making insights memorable. Research shows that data presented in narrative format is retained far better than raw statistics alone.

4. Three Key Characteristics Make It Powerful:
— It makes insights and key findings memorable to the audience
— It is a persuasive way of communicating key insights to both business and technical stakeholders
— It creates engagement — the story keeps the audience invested in the data's outcome

5. Bridging Technology and People: Data storytelling is filling a vital gap in business and in the technical analytics process between technology and people. By combining an increasingly essential resource (data) with a familiar, time-tested form of communication (storytelling), this skill helps more people translate their insights into action.

6. Brand and Customer Engagement: Companies like Spotify (Wrapped) and Uber (Year in Review) have demonstrated that personalised data stories generate massive engagement — turning dry usage statistics into emotional, shareable personal narratives. This has elevated data storytelling from an internal analytical skill to a consumer-facing marketing strategy.

Conclusion: From a data literacy perspective, once an organisation's people are comfortable with reading and working with data, they must also learn to communicate insights effectively. Data storytelling is no longer optional — it is an essential skill for anyone working with data in any professional context.
LQ 2. Explain the steps to create a Data Story. Use an example to illustrate each step.
Creating an effective data story follows four key steps. Using the example of analysing student exam performance across a school year:

Step 1 — Collect the Data and Organise It
Gather relevant, reliable data from appropriate sources and organise it systematically. Remove duplicates, clean missing values, and ensure accuracy. The quality of your story depends entirely on the quality of your data.
Example: Collect term-by-term exam scores for each subject across all students. Organise into a spreadsheet with columns: Student ID, Subject, Term 1 Score, Term 2 Score, Term 3 Score, Attendance %.

Step 2 — Use Proper Visualisation Tools to Visualise the Data
Select the right chart type for your data and create clear, labelled visualisations using appropriate tools (MS Excel, Tableau, Power BI).
Example: Create a Line Graph showing average scores by subject across three terms. Create a Scatter Plot of Attendance % vs. Final Score. Create a Bar Chart comparing subject-wise average scores. Each visualisation reveals a different aspect of the data.

Step 3 — Observe the Relationships Between the Data
Analyse the visualisations for trends, patterns, correlations, and anomalies. Ask what is changing, what is correlated, and where the surprise is in the data.
Example: The scatter plot shows a strong positive correlation — students with higher attendance consistently scored higher. The line graph shows that Science scores declined sharply in Term 2. These observations are the raw insights that will power the narrative.

Step 4 — Create a Simple Narrative Hidden in the Data
Craft a clear, structured narrative using Freytag's Pyramid framework, tailored to your audience's data literacy level.
Example Narrative: "Our students began the year strong, but a concerning dip in Science performance in Term 2 — driven by a period of low attendance during the rainy season — suggests that attendance directly impacts academic outcomes. Schools that implemented additional catch-up sessions recovered their scores in Term 3. Our recommendation: introduce a structured attendance intervention programme for at-risk students each monsoon term."

Key Principle: The narrative should communicate the insight, provide context, and end with a clear call to action. It should be crisp, factual, and tailored to the audience — avoiding technical jargon when presenting to non-technical stakeholders.
Section E — Case Study Questions
CS 1. A city government collected data on traffic accidents at intersections to identify high-risk areas and prioritise safety improvements. They developed a data storytelling report to present their findings to city officials. What is the primary purpose of this data storytelling report, and what elements should it include?
Primary Purpose: The primary purpose of the city government's data storytelling report is to identify high-risk areas for traffic accidents and propose targeted interventions to improve road safety — Option B from the original case study.

What the Report Should Include:

Data Element:
— Accident frequency data by intersection (last 3–5 years)
— Severity data (minor, major, fatal)
— Time-of-day and day-of-week patterns
— Road conditions, speed limits, and traffic volume at each intersection

Visual Element:
— Map Chart: geographic visualisation of accident hotspots (colour-coded by frequency/severity) — immediately shows city officials where the problem areas are
— Bar Chart: top 10 most dangerous intersections by accident count
— Line Graph: accident trends over time (are they increasing or decreasing?)
— Heat Map: accidents by time-of-day and day-of-week

Narrative Element (using Freytag's Pyramid):
— Introduction: Context — road safety challenge and data collected
— Rising Action: Evidence — showing the patterns and concentrations of accidents
— Climax: The key insight — e.g., "3 intersections account for 40% of all serious accidents, and 80% occur between 5–8pm on weekdays"
— Falling Action: Interpretation — cost of inaction, comparison with similar cities that improved safety
— Conclusion: Specific recommendations — install traffic signals, reduce speed limits, add pedestrian crossings at the top 3 intersections

Ethics to Consider: Ensure no individual accident victims are identifiable in the data; cite sources clearly; be transparent about any data limitations (e.g., underreporting).
